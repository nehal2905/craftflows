Why this matters

01 / Introduction
01

Where the hours actually go

Very little time is lost to big, visible failures. It disappears in small increments: re-typing a lead into the CRM, chasing a client for a document, assembling the same Monday report. Each task takes minutes. Together, they routinely consume 10–20% of a team’s working week, and because no single task feels expensive, nobody measures the total.

02

Repetitive work compounds

Manual processes scale linearly with growth: twice the clients means twice the onboarding emails, invoices, and status updates. Headcount usually grows slower than workload, so the gap is absorbed as overtime, delays, and errors. The processes that were “fine” at 5 employees quietly become the bottleneck at 20.

03

This is not about replacing people

Automation removes the work nobody was hired to do. Your accountant wasn’t hired to copy numbers between spreadsheets, and your account manager wasn’t hired to send reminder emails. Removing that layer returns people to the judgment-heavy work that actually justifies their role, and that clients actually pay for.

04

Small tasks first, always

The instinct is to automate the biggest, most complex process. In practice, the highest ROI comes from small, frequent, rule-based tasks: they’re cheap to automate, quick to ship, and their savings recur daily. A 10-minute task performed 15 times a week is 130 hours a year, from one workflow.

How to use this scorecard

  1. Run the 21-point audit (page 3) to see which standard workflows are still manual in your business.
  2. Inventory your own workflows (page 4) and quantify what they cost you every week (page 5).
  3. Score your top five against the priority matrix (page 6) and read your overall result (page 7).
  4. Plan the rollout on the roadmap (page 8), then read the field notes on page 9 before you build anything.

Before you automate

08 / Field notes

Six rules we apply on every build. They’re the difference between automation that quietly compounds and automation that quietly breaks.

RULE 01

Don’t automate broken processes

Automation is an amplifier. A messy workflow made faster is just mess at scale. If a process produces bad output today, fix the process first, then automate the fixed version.

RULE 02

Standardize first

If three people do the same task three different ways, there’s nothing to automate yet. Agree on the one right way, run it manually for two weeks, then encode it.

RULE 03

Document workflows

Write the steps down before building: every input, decision, and hand-off. Documentation surfaces the edge cases that break automations, while they’re still cheap to handle.

RULE 04

Measure current time spent

You can’t prove ROI without a baseline. Track a week of the manual version, honestly, so that six months from now the savings are a number, not a feeling.

RULE 05

Automate repetitive decisions

The biggest wins usually aren’t tasks but decisions made by rule: routing, approving under a threshold, prioritizing. If a decision follows “if X then Y,” software should be making it.

RULE 06

Review every automation quarterly

Tools change, teams change, edge cases accumulate. A 30-minute quarterly review of what’s running, and whether it still matches reality, keeps automations an asset instead of a liability.
